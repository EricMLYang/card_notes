Agents Today #16 - The Great Reshuffling: How AI is Polarizing Product Management Roles
After taking a few months away from writing, I've returned to find us in the midst of what I can only describe as a reshuffling, a polarization of PM roles that are creating winners and losers.
Mike Clark
Aug 18, 2025




Intro
The product management job market is experiencing a dramatic K-shaped split. High demand exists for AI-focused PMs building AI products and AI-powered PMs who've mastered AI tools, while traditional generalist PM roles are rapidly disappearing. This shift represents more than just new skill requirements because it's a fundamental cultural transformation where AI proficiency has evolved from "cheating" to an absolute requirement. Companies report that junior professionals leveraging AI are often outperforming senior staff who resist these tools, forcing a complete reevaluation of team structures and career progression. With forecasts predicting 23% of all jobs will change by 2027 due to AI, product managers serve as the canary in the coal mine for broader workforce transformation. The message is clear: adapt to working with AI agents or risk professional obsolescence.

Mike's Insights
Before diving into the data, I want to acknowledge that this transformation is happening faster than most of us anticipated. When I started writing about AI agents last year, I expected gradual adoption. Instead, we're witnessing a rapid bifurcation that's reshaping not just individual careers but entire organizational structures.

A number of companies I've spoken with over the past few months tell a consistent story: the most productive team members aren't necessarily the most experienced ones anymore. They're the ones who've learned to work effectively with AI. This isn't just about using AI to write emails, it's about fundamentally reimagining how product work gets done and products get built.

What makes this particularly fascinating is how it mirrors historical technology adoption patterns. Just as spreadsheets and calculators were once considered "cheating," AI tools are following the same trajectory from suspicion to requirement. The difference is the speed and scope of this transformation.

In this article, I'll examine four key dimensions of this shift: the polarization of PM roles, the cultural evolution from AI stigma to AI requirement, the emergence of AI as everyone's personal chief of staff, and the broader implications for team structures and the job market.

The K-Shaped Job Market: A Tale of Two Product Managers
The product management job market is experiencing an unprecedented polarization that's creating distinct winners and losers. Rather than a gradual evolution of existing roles, we're witnessing a sharp bifurcation that's fundamentally altering career trajectories.




The Upside-Down Bell Curve
I had been seeing a shift, but in a recent conversation with Minal Mehta (LinkedIn) she observed this upside-down bell curve happening with PM roles right now. Traditional hiring patterns in product management followed a relatively normal distribution, with the majority of openings targeting mid-level generalist PMs. Today's market looks dramatically different. The product management job market is showing a K-shaped pattern, with surging demand at two extremes while the middle hollows out.

On the far right of this curve are AI-focused PMs as specialists building AI-driven products, managing machine learning features, and navigating the complex intersection of data science and product strategy. These roles command premium salaries, with a few recent analysis showing AI product managers earning approximately 35% more than their traditional counterparts, yet 75% of employers struggle to find qualified candidates.

On the far left are AI-powered PMs, practitioners who have fully embraced AI tools in every facet of their workflow. These professionals leverage AI for research, documentation, analysis, and strategic planning, achieving productivity levels and time to impact that traditional PMs simply cannot match.

The middle ground - traditional product management roles without an AI component - is rapidly disappearing. Experience with large language models has become one of the top three hard skills hiring managers now look for in PM candidates. Traditional PM job postings that don't mention AI or data skills have become increasingly rare, replaced by postings that explicitly call for AI familiarity.

The Numbers Tell the Story
By late 2024, over 5,700 PM jobs were open globally, representing a recovery from 2023 lows. However, the composition of these openings reveals the transformation: entry- and mid-level traditional openings made up a smaller share than before, while specialized AI roles and senior positions requiring AI proficiency dominated the landscape.

More than one in five open PM roles are now concentrated in the San Francisco Bay Area, where AI expertise commands the highest premiums. The fastest-growing skill tags in PM job descriptions include SQL proficiency and LLM experience, technical capabilities that were once considered "nice-to-have" but are now fundamental requirements.

This polarization creates a stark reality: being neither an AI specialist nor an AI-proficient generalist is becoming a career liability. The middle ground that once provided stable employment for thousands of product managers is rapidly eroding.

From Stigma to Requirement: The Cultural Revolution
Perhaps the most remarkable aspect of this transformation is the speed at which organizational culture has shifted regarding AI usage. What, only a short time ago, was once considered "cheating" has become not just acceptable, but mandatory for competitive performance.




The Cheating Stigma
As recently as late 2024, nearly half of 17,000 surveyed desk workers said they'd feel uncomfortable telling their manager they used AI, with the top reason being that using AI feels like cheating. Almost as many worried their boss would think they're less competent. This stigma echoed the early days of calculators in mathematics education or spreadsheets in financial analysis where tools that were initially viewed with suspicion before becoming indispensable.

The parallel to historical tech adoption is striking. Calculators were once banned from math classrooms because educators worried students wouldn't learn fundamental arithmetic. Spreadsheets faced resistance in accounting departments where manual ledgers were considered more trustworthy. In both cases, the tools eventually proved to enhance rather than replace human capability, enabling people to tackle more complex problems by automating routine calculations.

The Rapid Flip
By 2025, this dynamic had completely reversed. A Microsoft Work Trend Index report found that 75% of workers are already using AI at work, with a remarkable 80% bringing their own AI tools into the workplace within the past year. The attitude from leadership evolved dramatically: two-thirds of business leaders now say they wouldn't hire someone without AI skills, and 71% would rather choose a less-experienced candidate with strong AI skills over a more experienced candidate without them.

This cultural shift has created some fascinating upheavals in team dynamics. Many seasoned PMs and engineers initially resisted integrating AI into their daily work, whether out of skepticism or habit. Meanwhile, junior professionals who recently started their career grew up with AI began embracing these tools to draft Product Requirement Documents, generate prototypes, analyze user data, and accelerate their learning cycles.

The Generational Performance Flip
The result has been an inversion of traditional productivity hierarchies. In some startups, junior team members armed with AI are consistently out-delivering veterans who stick to older methods. Leaders have noticed that those hesitant to use AI end up losing efficiency, fundamentally changing who gets opportunities and advancement.

This shift is quantified in executive attitudes: 77% of executives report that with AI to support work, they are now willing to give bigger responsibilities to earlier-career talent. The logic is compelling - one junior professional with AI can often accomplish work that previously required a whole support team.

The message from this cultural transformation is unambiguous: using AI on the job isn't "cheating," rather it's the price of admission for modern productivity. Much like Excel proficiency in the 2000s or internet literacy in the 2010s, AI competency in 2025 is simply table stakes for knowledge work.

Everyone Gets an AI Chief of Staff
One of the most transformative aspects of the current AI revolution is how it's democratizing access to executive level support. What was once the exclusive privilege of C-suite executives. Having a dedicated chief of staff to manage information, coordinate activities, and provide strategic counsel is now available to every knowledge worker.




The Promise Realized
Bill Gates predicted that "in the next five years, anyone who's online will be able to have a personal assistant powered by AI" that can handle a wide range of tasks. That future has arrived faster than anticipated. The connections to your data on major platforms have rolled out AI copilots, from Microsoft 365 Copilot to Google's Gemini in Workspace, that integrate directly with calendars, emails, documents, and communication tools.

These AI agents function as proactive digital assistants, learning individual work patterns and preferences to provide increasingly sophisticated support. They can manage schedules, draft communications, capture meeting transcripts, prioritize action items, and handle routine coordination with minimal human input.

One of my co-workers, Julia Wiesinger (LinkedIn), recently shared with a bunch of us her own personal and professional Chief's of Staff she had assembled for managing her daily life. Tal Laviv (Lenny's Newsletter) published an article last month on building your own co-pilot as a guide to using AI as a long-term thinking partner. I've had my library of prompts the last few years and this slight mental shift in how to align my workflow and context into a Gemini Gem to be my chief of staff has been rocket fuel for a number of things I have needed to work through recently.

Real-World Impact
The productivity gains are measurable and significant. Early studies by McKinsey found that product managers using generative AI were able to complete certain deliverables 30% faster without sacrificing quality when a senior PM oversaw the output. Junior PMs showed even more dramatic improvements, though both human and techinical quality control remained essential.

One PM describes their AI agent as "a virtual intern that helps filter out the noise so I can focus on the stuff that actually matters." This sentiment captures the essence of the transformation that AI isn't replacing human judgment but rather handling the information processing and routine coordination that previously consumed significant cognitive bandwidth and time.

The key to success lies in treating AI like a true chief of staff rather than a simple automation tool. This means providing clear instructions, establishing workflows for review and refinement, and maintaining human oversight of outputs. Those who master this collaborative approach report feeling less overwhelmed and more in control of their daily responsibilities.

The Learning Curve
Having an AI helper is only valuable if you know how to use it effectively. "Nobody wants to generate AI slop," as critics rightly point out, poorly guided AI output can create more work than it saves. A new skill set has emerged around prompt crafting, workflow integration, and quality control of AI-generated content. Not just better prompts. Actually custom engineered context for your part of the world.

The most successful practitioners treat their AI agents as senior-level collaborators, providing full context and strategic direction rather than simple task assignments. They've learned to iterate collaboratively, using AI to generate initial drafts and ideas that they then refine and validate through human judgment.

The Great Restructuring: New Teams for a New Era



The integration of AI agents is sending shockwaves through traditional team structures, creating new roles while potentially eliminating others. The implications extend far beyond individual productivity gains to fundamental questions about talent development, career progression, and organizational design.

The Junior Talent Crisis
One of the most concerning trends emerging from this transformation is the potential erosion of entry-level learning opportunities. AI coding assistants and other generative tools are becoming increasingly proficient at handling tasks that have traditionally served as training grounds for junior engineers and product managers - writing basic code, fixing simple bugs, creating initial documentation, and conducting preliminary research.

A 2025 survey of over 880 engineering leaders revealed that a majority (54%) expect AI to reduce their long-term hiring of junior engineers. This creates what economists call a "tragedy of the commons" scenario: while it may appear cost-effective for individual companies to leverage AI rather than invest in training junior employees, if every company adopts this approach, the industry-wide pipeline of experienced professionals will eventually dry up.

The productivity gains from AI are also creating pressure for faster project turnaround times, with 39% of engineering leaders expecting this acceleration. Combined with the automation of junior tasks, this is fostering teams that are leaner, more senior-heavy, and increasingly reliant on AI agents to function as "virtual junior developers."

The Emergence of Agent Operations
As AI agents become integral team members, organizations are creating entirely new roles to manage and optimize them. Deloitte analysts predict that by 2026, organizations will establish dedicated "agent ops" teams - staff who monitor, train, and govern fleets of AI agents to ensure they're working correctly and ethically.

Much like DevOps teams evolved to maintain software pipelines, AgentOps teams will be responsible for the performance of AI coworkers. This includes roles like prompt engineers who optimize AI interactions, AI ethicists who ensure responsible deployment, and AI trainers who adjust agent behavior and fix mistakes.

Nearly half of LinkedIn respondents believe autonomous AI agents will "significantly transform" their organization in the next 2-3 years, implying that companies will need new frameworks and dedicated personnel to manage this transformation.

The Management Evolution
As tech writer Shaan Puri observed, "in the future being able to 'manage robots' will be more valuable than managing humans." This insight captures a fundamental shift in management requirements. Future leaders won't just manage people - they'll manage people plus AI agents, requiring new skills in human-AI collaboration and coordination.

Companies like Salesforce are already reporting that AI automation is handling 30-40% of all work internally, effectively meaning every team has a significant portion of work done by AI helpers. This is creating flatter organizational structures as AI takes over layers of coordination and middle management functions.

The most successful teams are adopting "human-in-the-loop" setups where humans handle exceptions and final approvals while AI manages routine execution. This requires new best practices around AI oversight, quality control, and accountability when agents make mistakes.

Economic Forecasts: The Great Realignment



Looking ahead to 2026-2027, economic forecasts paint a picture of massive workforce transformation rather than simple job displacement. The data suggests we're entering a period of "Great Realignment" where skills matter more than experience and adaptability trumps tenure.

The Numbers Game
According to the World Economic Forum, nearly a quarter (23%) of all jobs are expected to change by 2027 due to AI and automation. This doesn't mean all those jobs will disappear - rather, their fundamental nature will shift. WEF forecasts about 69 million new roles will be created by 2027, while 83 million will be displaced, resulting in a net loss of approximately 14 million jobs globally - about 2% of the workforce.

These projections are echoed by other major research institutions. Goldman Sachs analysts estimate that generative AI could increase global GDP by 7% (or nearly $7 trillion) over the next decade by boosting productivity. However, they also forecast a 6-7% displacement risk for specific, highly automatable roles such as administrative assistants, proofreaders, and some computer programmers.

The Skills Chasm
The central challenge emerging from these forecasts isn't mass unemployment but a severe skills mismatch. Bain & Company projects the creation of 2.3 million AI-specific jobs by 2027, but estimates the available talent pool will only grow to 1.2 million - creating a massive shortfall of over one million qualified professionals.

This skills gap is already visible in current hiring patterns. The WEF identifies this mismatch as the single greatest barrier to business transformation for most organizations. The defining economic problem of the next decade will be the disconnect between the skills demanded by the new economy and those possessed by the current workforce.

Divergent Expert Predictions
Forecasts vary widely on the ultimate impact. Some experts issue stark warnings - venture capitalist Kai-Fu Lee cautions that AI could displace up to 50% of jobs by 2027 if adoption accelerates unchecked. Dario Amodei, CEO of Anthropic, has warned of a potential "white-collar bloodbath" where half of entry-level white-collar roles vanish within five years.

Others argue AI will create more jobs than it destroys in the long run, following historical patterns of technological disruption. One study by OpenAI researchers found that 80% of the U.S. workforce could have at least 10% of their tasks affected by AI, and nearly 1 in 5 workers might see 50% of their tasks automated - but this doesn't necessarily translate to job elimination.

What's clear across all forecasts is that virtually every job will evolve. The question isn't whether AI will impact your role, but how quickly you can adapt to working alongside AI agents.

Implications for Product Professionals



For product managers and aspiring PMs, these trends create both unprecedented opportunities and existential risks. The path forward requires strategic thinking about skill development, career positioning, and organizational adaptation.

The Adaptation Imperative
The data makes one thing abundantly clear: AI proficiency is no longer optional for product management careers. Whether you're building AI products or using AI tools, competency in human-AI collaboration has become as fundamental as understanding agile methodologies or user research principles.

This doesn't mean every PM needs to become a machine learning engineer. Rather, it means developing fluency in AI capabilities and limitations, learning to work effectively with AI tools, and understanding how to integrate AI insights into product strategy and execution.

Career Positioning Strategies
For current PMs, the choice is stark: specialize in AI product management or become exceptionally proficient at AI-augmented product management. The middle ground of traditional generalist roles is rapidly disappearing.

AI specialization requires developing technical literacy in machine learning concepts, data pipeline management, and AI ethics. It means understanding model evaluation metrics, data quality requirements, and the unique challenges of probabilistic systems.

AI augmentation focuses on mastering the tools and workflows that amplify human productivity. This includes prompt engineering, AI-assisted research and analysis, automated documentation generation, and strategic use of AI for competitive intelligence and market research.

Organizational Readiness
For product leaders and executives, the transformation requires proactive cultural and structural changes. Organizations must invest in upskilling programs, democratize access to AI tools, and create psychological safety for experimentation and learning.

The companies that successfully navigate this transition will be those that embrace AI as a force multiplier rather than a replacement for human judgment. They'll create "bridge" programs to help traditional PMs develop AI competencies and establish new career pathways that blend human insight with AI capability.

The Road Ahead



The transformation of product management roles represents just the beginning of a broader workforce evolution. As AI agents become more sophisticated and ubiquitous, every knowledge-based profession will face similar pressures to adapt or risk obsolescence.

The product management community has always prided itself on being at the intersection of technology, business, and user needs. Today, that intersection includes artificial intelligence as a fundamental component. The PMs who thrive in this new landscape will be those who learn to orchestrate human creativity and AI capability in service of better products and user experiences.

The message is clear: the age of AI agents isn't coming... it's here. The question isn't whether to adapt, but how quickly you can embrace the tools and mindsets that will define the future of product work.

Conclusion
The polarization of product management roles represents more than a job market shift, it's a fundamental restructuring of how product work gets done. The emergence of AI-focused specialists and AI-augmented generalists, combined with the disappearance of traditional middle-ground roles, signals a new era where AI competency isn't just valuable - it's essential for career survival.

The cultural transformation from viewing AI as "cheating" to treating it as a requirement mirrors historical technology adoption patterns, but with unprecedented speed and scope. Organizations that successfully navigate this transition will be those that invest in their people's AI literacy while maintaining focus on uniquely human capabilities like strategic vision, ethical judgment, and empathetic leadership.

As we look toward 2026-2027, the forecasts point to massive job transformation rather than simple displacement. The winners will be those who learn to work effectively with AI agents, while those who resist this collaboration risk being left behind by more adaptable competitors.

The future of product management isn't about humans versus AI, it's about humans with AI creating better products, faster insights, and more innovative solutions than either could achieve alone. The Great Reshuffling is underway, and the time to adapt is now.
